Aretha Franklin

The Queen of Soul

Aretha Louise Franklin was an American singer, songwriter and pianist. Honored as the "Queen of Soul", Rolling Stone magazine twice named her as the greatest singer of all time. As a child, Franklin was noticed for her gospel singing at New Bethel Baptist Church in Detroit, Michigan, where her father C. L. Franklin was a minister.

Here's a condensed time line of Aretha Louise Franklin's life:

  • 1942 - Born on March 25 in Memphis, Tennessee, U.S.
  • 1952 - Learned how to play piano by ear.
  • 1956 - J.V.B. Records released Franklin's first single, "Never Grow Old", backed with "You Grow Closer".
  • 1958 - Went on tour with Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.
  • 1960 - "Today I Sing the Blues", was issued in September and later reached the top 10 of the Hot Rhythm & Blues Sellers chart.
  • 1961 - In January, Columbia issued Franklin's first album, Aretha: With The Ray Bryant Combo. The album featured her first single to chart the Billboard Hot 100, "Won't Be Long", which also peaked at number 7 on the R&B chart. By the end of the year, Franklin was named as a "new-star female vocalist" in DownBeat magazine.
  • 1965 - Checker Records released “Songs of Faith” album (Aretha was only 14 when Songs of Faith was recorded).
  • 1966 - She had two R&B charted singles, with the songs "One Step Ahead" and "Cry Like a Baby", while also reaching the Easy Listening charts with the ballads "You Made Me Love You" and "(No, No) I'm Losing You". By the mid-1960s, Franklin made $100,000 annually from countless performances in nightclubs and theaters.
  • 1967 - "I Never Loved a Man (The Way I Love You)" was released and reached number one on the R&B chart, peaking at number nine on the Billboard Hot 100, giving Franklin her first top-ten pop single. The song's B-side, "Do Right Woman, Do Right Man", reached the R&B Top 40, peaking at number 37. "Respect" was Otis Redding's song, but Aretha modified it with a "supercharged interlude featuring the emphatic spelling-out of the song's title". Her frenetic version was released in April and reached number one on both the R&B and pop charts. "Respect" became her signature song. Franklin also scored two additional top-ten singles, "Baby I Love You" and "(You Make Me Feel Like) A Natural Woman".
  • 1968 - Franklin issued the top-selling albums “Lady Soul and Aretha Now”, which included some of her most popular hit singles, including "Chain of Fools", "Ain't No Way", "Think", and "I Say a Little Prayer". And Franklin earned the first two of her Grammys, including the debut category for Best Female R&B Vocal Performance. On February 16, Franklin was honored with a day named for her and was greeted by longtime friend Martin Luther King Jr., who gave her the SCLC Drum Beat Award for Musicians two months before his death. Franklin sang at Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.'s funeral.
  • 1969 - In March, Franklin was unanimously voted winner of Académie du Jazz's R&B award, Prix Otis Redding, for her albums “Lady Soul, Aretha Now”, and “Aretha in Paris”.
  • 1971 - Became the first R&B performer to headline Fillmore West, later that year releasing the live album Aretha Live at Fillmore West.
  • 1972 - In January, she returned to Gospel music in a two-night, live-church recording, with the album “Amazing Grace”, which sold more than two million copies, and is one of the best-selling gospel albums of all time.
  • 1979 - Received a star on the Hollywood Walk of Fame.
  • 1980 - Gave a command performance at London's Royal Albert Hall in front of Queen Elizabeth The Queen Mother. And Franklin's album, Aretha, featured the number-three R&B hit "United Together" and her Grammy-nominated cover of Redding's "I Can't Turn You Loose".
  • 1982 -Achieved a gold record—for the first time in seven years—with the album "Jump to It". The album's title track was her first top-40 single on the pop charts in six years.
  • 1985 - Had her voice declared a Michigan "natural resource".
  • 1987 - Became the first woman inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame.
  • 1991 - The National Academy of Recording Arts and Sciences awarded her a Grammy Legend Award.
  • 1999 - Recipient of the National Medal of Arts.
  • 2003 - "So Damn Happy", was released and featured the Grammy-winning song "Wonderful".
  • 2005 - Recipient of the American Academy of Achievement's Golden Plate Award and was bestowed the Presidential Medal of Freedom by then-President George W. Bush. She was inducted into the Michigan Rock and Roll Legends Hall of Fame.
  • 2008 -  Was honored as the MusiCares Person of the Year, and performed "Never Gonna Break My Faith", which had won her the Grammy for best Gospel performance the year before.
  • 2009 - On January 20, Franklin made international headlines for performing "My Country, 'Tis of Thee" at President Barack Obama's inaugural ceremony with her church hat becoming a popular topic online.
  • 2010 - Accepted one of her honorary degrees, this one from Yale University.
  • 2014 - She became the first woman to have 100 songs on Billboard's Hot R&B/Hip-Hop Songs chart with the success of her cover of Adele's "Rolling in the Deep", which debuted at number 47 on the chart.
  • 2017 - Franklin released the album "A Brand New Me", in November, with the Royal Philharmonic Orchestra. It peaked at number five on the Billboard Top Classical Albums chart before her death and rose to number two after her death. Franklin's final public performance was at the Cathedral of St. John the Divine in New York City during Elton John's 25th anniversary gala for the Elton John AIDS Foundation on November 7.
  • 2018 - Franklin died on August 16, at the age of 76.

“Nobody embodies more fully the connection between the African-American spiritual, the blues, R. & B., rock and roll—the way that hardship and sorrow were transformed into something full of beauty and vitality and hope. American history wells up when Aretha sings. That's why, when she sits down at a piano and sings 'A Natural Woman,' she can move me to tears—the same way that Ray Charles's version of 'America the Beautiful' will always be in my view the most patriotic piece of music ever performed—because it captures the fullness of the American experience, the view from the bottom as well as the top, the good and the bad, and the possibility of synthesis, reconciliation, transcendence.”

-- President Barack Obama
